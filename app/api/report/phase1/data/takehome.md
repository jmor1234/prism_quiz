# Take-Home Health Tests

## 1. Resting Pulse Test

**Instructions:** Count how many times your heart beats over the course of one minute by placing your finger(s) on the vein nearest your wrist. Repeat this once per day at the same time (mid morning or afternoon) and record the values of how many beats per minute here for 4 consecutive days.

**Interpretation:** The resting pulse rate should be between 70 and 90 beats per minute, indicating optimal metabolic function. It is important to note that the temperature and pulse can both be elevated by stress hormones such as cortisol or adrenaline, so it should be considered alongside other signs of stress.

## 2. Fingernail and Hair Assessment

**Instructions:** Please take pictures of your fingernails and hair.

**Interpretation:** An excess of estrogen can cause dry and brittle connective tissues, like the hair and skin.

---

## 3. Urination Frequency Test

**Instructions:** Count how many times you urinate per day for 4 consecutive days.

**Interpretation:** Frequent urination is often a sign of elevated "fight or flight" hormones, such as adrenaline, noradrenaline and parathyroid hormone.

---

## 4. Foamy Urine Assessment

**Instructions:** Take notice of your urine immediately after you use the bathroom for 4 days. Does it tend to be foamy?

**Interpretation:** Fatty acids, bile salts and some steroid hormones can be excreted through the urine in response to stress, bacterial overgrowths or detoxification, which may make the urine more soap-like in consistency, resulting in foamy urine. Foamy urine can also be the result of excess protein being liberated into the urine, typically as a result of kidney damage.

---

## 5. Bowel Movement Frequency Test

**Instructions:** Please count how many times you defecate over the course of 4 days.

**Interpretation:** 
- **Infrequent bowel movements:** The inability to have complete and frequent bowel movements is driven by a lack of adequate bowel contractions or improper hydration of stool in the colon. These can be caused by bacterial or fungal overgrowths, low stomach acid, deficiencies of key nutrients, excess serotonin and hypothyroidism.
- **Very frequent bowel movements:** Having very frequent bowel movements can be a sign of excess bacterial growth leading to a large fecal load, and enhanced urgency due to stress signals like adrenaline and serotonin.

---

## 6. Bristol Stool Scale Assessment

**Instructions:** What Types on the Bristol Stool Scale did each of your bowel movements fall under?

**Interpretation:** 
- **Type 1-2 stools:** Having type 1-2 stools is typically associated with a lack of colonic hydration, potentially due to magnesium deficiency, or poor motility and inflammation due to irritating foods or bacterial overgrowth.
- **Type 5-7 stools:** Having type 5-7 stools is a key sign of inflammation or bacterial overgrowth, and can be the result of excess serotonin production.
- **Undigested food:** Undigested food is typically a sign of low stomach acid or bacterial overgrowth, as proper acid secretion and microbial fermentation is necessary to properly digest and assimilate food.

---

## 7. Barnes Basal Temperature Test

**Instructions:** Place a thermometer (any kind will do) in the armpit and leave it there for 10 minutes upon waking in the morning. The test should be done on 2 consecutive days to ensure an accurate measurement. **WOMEN ONLY:** Please indicate (approximately) what date your last period began.

**Interpretation:** Broda Barnes, MD, became famous for his simple and effective method for diagnosing hypothyroidism. Even in the absence of overt thyroid hormone abnormalities in the blood, people can still experience hypothyroid symptoms. Barnes noted that a low morning temperature (under 97.8°F or 36.6°C) was the most reliable predictor of whether a patient would benefit from thyroid therapy.

---

## 8. Tongue Assessment

**Instructions:** Take a picture of your tongue upon waking in the morning on 4 consecutive mornings under neutral lighting.

**Interpretation:** A coated tongue, or oral thrush, is typically caused by a fungal or bacterial overgrowth that originates in the gut. It can also be caused by reverse peristalsis, the improper motility of the gastrointestinal system up instead of down, resulting in the bacteria or fungi moving up towards the mouth and presenting on the tongue.
