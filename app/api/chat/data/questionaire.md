## 6. Sleep Patterns and Quality

**Questions:** 
- Do you tend to wake up unintentionally in the middle of the night when sleeping?
- In which part of the night do you tend to wake up unintentionally?
- Why do you wake up unintentionally?
- Do you find that you are hot or sweaty when you wake up unintentionally?
- Do you dream?
- Do you experience nightmares or sleep paralysis?
- Do you have trouble falling asleep?
- Do you struggle to get a restful and restorative sleep?

**Implications:** 

**Waking timing and causes:** Waking within the first few hours of falling asleep can be due to inappropriate elevations in stress hormones such as adrenaline, cortisol and estrogen. Waking up later on in the night could be a sign of a gut disturbance, as undigested food and bacteria can stimulate the central nervous system.

**Reasons for waking:**
- **To urinate:** May be a result of poor salt intake or retention, a disrupted sleep schedule or low zinc, vitamin C or copper intake. These nutrients tell the body to stop urinating throughout the night, through hormones called anti-diuretic hormone and adrenaline.
- **To eat something:** Could indicate poor liver function, since this is the organ that stores carbs that provide energy throughout the night. Night time hypoglycemia can activate stress systems, causing awakening and cravings for carbohydrates and salty food. Bacterial endotoxin is primarily processed by the liver, and can damage it with enough exposure.
- **For no apparent reason:** Often driven by intestinal irritation, typically by indigestible food or bacteria.

**Hot and sweaty:** Feeling hot and sweaty at night can be a result of rising estrogen during sleep.

**Dreaming:** Dreaming is a sign that the sleep is deep and restorative, which is indicative of good health and metabolism.

**Nightmares:** Regular nightmares are a sign of night time stress, and may be driven by hormones like cortisol, serotonin and noradrenaline. This may go hand in hand with hypothyroidism.

**Trouble falling asleep:** May be a result of too much artificial light exposure, a poor sleep schedule, or elevations in stress hormones like adrenaline, estrogen and cortisol at night.

**Non-restful sleep:** Sleep that is not restful can be indicative of excess serotonin, which should be converted into melatonin, producing restful sleep. An excess of serotonin may make you drowsy or sleepy, but it promotes an energy conserving hibernation state instead of a restorative sleep.

Sugar cravings in general should not be considered pathological, though, as a lack of sugar preference is common in depression and aging.

It is also typically associated with constipation or incomplete bowel movements, especially when accompanied by bloating.

## 31. Immediate Post-Meal Symptoms

**Question:** Do symptoms worsen *immediately* after eating?

**Implication:** Food first starts getting broken down by the stomach, so symptoms immediately upon eating can be indicative of an issue with the stomach, such as low stomach acid or poor gastric motility.

---

## 32. Symptoms 1-3 Hours Post-Meal

**Question:** Do symptoms worsen 1-3 hours after eating?

**Implication:** Food gets absorbed in the small intestine during this time frame, so the associated symptoms can be indicative of an issue in this region, such as bacterial or fungal overgrowths, poor bile acid and digestive enzyme secretion.

---

## 33. Symptom Relief After Bowel Movement

**Question:** Do symptoms alleviate after a bowel movement?

**Implication:** The presence of stool in the GI tract is sufficient to cause elevations in stress mediators like serotonin, which can cause mood and digestive symptoms, so its elimination can indicate potential pathological pathways.
