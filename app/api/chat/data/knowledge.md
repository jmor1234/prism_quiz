The gut is so much more than a hub for digestion. It's the epicenter of our immune system and its breakdown is the main driver of chronic inflammation throughout the body.

### The Gut: More Than Just Digestion

The gut is far more than a digestive tube - it's a central control hub for inflammation, immune activity, neurotransmission, and hormonal signaling. Most importantly, these actions in the gut can impair energy metabolism throughout the body, leading to seemingly unrelated symptoms.

**Key Insight**: When your gut is unhealthy, it can manifest as symptoms throughout your entire body, often in ways you wouldn't expect.

These seemingly unrelated symptoms may all stem from gut dysfunction. Click on any condition to learn more.

#### 1. Depression

Mood disturbances are among the most common symptoms of gut dysfunction. Studies show individuals with major depressive disorder often exhibit elevated markers of dysbiosis intestinal inflammation, and leaky gut.

Inflammatory cytokines and microbial metabolites directly affect serotonin and dopamine signaling in the brain. The gut microbiome produces about 90% of the body's serotonin, a key neurotransmitter that regulates mood.

**Clinical Insight**: In clinical practice, addressing gut health often leads to significant improvements in depressive symptoms, sometimes more effectively than antidepressant medications alone.

Anxiety is frequently rooted in gut-brain axis disruption. In clinical practice, it's striking how often anxiety improves with resolution of digestive issues.

Inflammatory signaling, vagus nerve irritation, and microbial imbalance all play a role. The vagus nerve serves as a direct communication pathway between the gut and brain, carrying signals in both directions.

**Research Finding**: Studies have shown that probiotics (particularly certain strains of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium) can reduce anxiety-like behavior in animal models and improve anxiety symptoms in humans.

#### 3. Brain Fog

The hallmark "foggy" thinking and poor focus often stem from gut bacterial overgrowth. Certain strains overproduce D-lactic acid, a neurotoxin that impairs cognition and mimics symptoms of intoxication.

The inflammation that bad bacteria and leaky gut can cause can reach the brain, worsening mental performance. This is sometimes referred to as "neuroinflammation."

**Treatment Note**: Targeted antimicrobials can significantly improve mental clarity. Many patients report dramatic improvements in cognitive function after treating small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO).

#### 4. Chronic Fatigue

Fatigue is a natural consequence of a gut under stress. Intestinal permeability, endotoxemia, and microbial imbalance all increase systemic inflammation and energy drain.

Strong correlations exist between gut inflammation markers and fatigue severity. The gut is responsible for absorbing nutrients that fuel our mitochondria (the energy powerhouses of our cells).

**Key Connection**: Many cases of chronic fatigue syndrome improve dramatically when gut health is addressed, particularly when addressing conditions like SIBO, Candida overgrowth, or intestinal permeability.

#### 5. Exercise Intolerance

Unusual fatigue during workouts can originate in the gut. Microbial endotoxins interfere with mitochondrial energy production and increase oxidative stress, making recovery harder and performance worse — even in well-trained individuals.

During intense exercise, blood flow is diverted from the gut to working muscles, which can lead to gut barrier dysfunction in susceptible individuals.

**Athlete Insight**: Many elite athletes struggle with gut issues that impair performance. Addressing gut health can lead to significant improvements in endurance, recovery, and overall athletic performance.

No supplement can fix poor sleep if the gut is inflamed. Bacterial overgrowth, especially in the small intestine, can irritate the gut lining and activate the nervous system — disrupting deep sleep cycles.

The gut microbiome follows a circadian rhythm and produces sleep-regulating neurotransmitters like GABA, serotonin, and melatonin.

**Treatment Note**: Antimicrobial therapies have shown improvements in sleep quality. Many patients report sleeping more deeply and waking more refreshed after addressing gut dysbiosis.

#### 7. Weight Gain

Gut dysfunction can promote metabolic disease. Studies show endotoxin exposure (as seen with increased gut permeability) induces insulin resistance and weight gain in animals.

Gut inflammation also boosts peripheral serotonin, which contributes to fat storage. Certain gut bacteria are associated with increased calorie extraction from food.

**Key Finding**: Fecal microbiota transplants from lean donors to obese recipients have shown to improve metabolic parameters, suggesting gut bacteria play a direct role in weight regulation.

The gut-skin axis is well documented. Psoriasis is strongly linked to intestinal permeability, and often co-occurs with GI symptoms like bloating, reflux, and diarrhea.

Improving gut health frequently leads to reduced skin inflammation. Many psoriasis patients report significant skin improvements when following gut-healing protocols.

**Clinical Note**: Psoriasis patients often have elevated markers of gut permeability and dysbiosis, even when they don't report digestive symptoms, highlighting the gut-skin connection.

Children born via C-section (who miss out on important gut bacteria from the birth canal) have higher rates of eczema, highlighting the early gut-skin connection.

**Research Insight**: Probiotic supplementation during pregnancy and infancy has been shown to reduce the risk of eczema development, suggesting gut microbiome plays a key role in skin health.

## The Good News

With the right approach, gut dysbiosis can often be corrected, leading to significant improvements in energy, digestion, and overall health.

## How Do You Know If You Have Dysbiosis?

Two simple, non-invasive clues that often indicate dysbiosis:

- **A coated tongue in the morning** (particularly at the back)
- **Consistently foamy urine**

If you notice these signs—especially alongside digestive symptoms—dysbiosis is likely at play.

## Diagnostic Testing

While stool tests can provide insight, they often lack consistency and clinical utility. High-quality testing is expensive and frequently does not alter the course of treatment. There are exceptions, but in most cases, symptoms and response to intervention are more telling.

## Understanding the Stress Response

Stress is not just psychological—it's a deeply biological process that affects every system in the body. Whether you struggle with high cortisol, anxiety, poor sleep, or persistent fatigue, chronic stress may be at the root.


## Common Symptoms of Chronic Stress

Many symptoms are commonly overlooked but often trace back to excessive or prolonged stress. These include:


### Sleep Issues

- Difficulty falling asleep
- Waking during the night
- Nightmares or sleep paralysis

### Physical Symptoms

- Foamy urine
- Hair loss or premature graying
- Abdominal weight gain

### Mental & Emotional

- Irritability, anger, or aggression
- Jitters, anxiety, lack of confidence
- Difficulty breathing or pounding heartbeat

### Digestive Issues

- Low or excessive appetite
- Gut pain, diarrhea, acid reflux
- Headaches, nausea, or constipation

### Hormonal

- Menstrual irregularities or cramps
- Cold extremities (fingers, nose, toes)
- Frequent urination

### Simple Indicators

- A pounding pulse
- Sense of pressure in the chest
- The "pupil test" for sympathetic activation

## The Biological Stress System

Stress perception begins in the brain. The amygdala processes emotional threats, while the hypothalamus initiates the physiological stress response through three primary pathways:


### HPA Axis

Releasing CRH to signal the pituitary, which produces ACTH and triggers cortisol production from the adrenal cortex

### Adrenal Medulla

Activating the brainstem and spinal cord to stimulate the adrenal medulla, releasing adrenaline and noradrenaline

### Sympathetic Nerves

Sending sympathetic nerve signals throughout the body

These mechanisms are designed for acute survival but become harmful when chronically activated.


### Glucose and the Stress Cascade

Low blood sugar is a powerful activator of the stress response. Hypothalamic neurons sense glucose deficiency and increase CRH, stimulating:


- Cortisol (HPA axis)
- Adrenaline (sympathetic nervous system)
- Glucagon release to generate glucose

This is why consuming carbohydrates or sugar rapidly lowers stress hormones, while carbohydrate restriction intensifies them.


### Caloric Restriction as a Stressor

Under-eating or fasting increases stress hormones. Stress eating exists for a reason: food availability signals safety. While overeating is not the goal, avoiding chronic caloric restriction is critical for managing stress.


Key Insight: The body interprets food scarcity as a threat, triggering stress responses.


## Key Anti-Stress Nutrients

- Supports adrenal function

### Zinc

- Suppresses cortisol by ~70% at 50 mg

### Magnesium

- Lowers cortisol and IL-6
- Calms the nervous system
- Commonly depleted in stressed individuals

### Vitamin E

- Improves blood pressure (a stress-sensitive metric)
- Anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective

### Vitamin D

- Decreases cortisol by 40% at 2,000 IU
- Suppresses inflammation in the brain and adrenals

### Sodium

- Low-salt diets activate stress systems
- Higher salt intake significantly reduces adrenaline
- 5,175 mg sodium reduced adrenaline by 50% compared to 1,150 mg

## Lifestyle Tools to Lower Stress

### Bright Light Exposure

Daytime light exposure helps reduce cortisol. Dim lighting prolongs stress hormone secretion.


### Nature Exposure

Being in nature lowers cortisol and stress perception. Negative ions from water, trees, and soil reduce sympathetic activity.


### Social Interaction

Reduces cortisol and prolactin, raises dopamine. Lack of connection promotes chronic stress physiology—prioritize real connection.

## Additional Physiological Drivers of Stress

### Gut Health

Gut nerves signal directly to the brain. Dysbiosis drives inflammation, estrogen, and serotonin—each of which fuels stress.


### Serotonin

Released from the gut in response to irritation. Stimulates the hypothalamus and increases cortisol. Tools to lower serotonin include ginger, lysine, vitamin D, zinc, and cyproheptadine.


### Histamine

Promotes inflammation and stress signaling. Modulated by vitamin C, DAO, ginger, L. plantarum, skullcap, quercetin, and carnosine.

Stimulates HPA axis activity. Opposed by progesterone, vitamin A, vitamin E, and aspirin.


### Prolactin

Increases under chronic stress and promotes cortisol release. Regulated by dopamine—supporting dopamine lowers stress.


### Lactic Acid

Causes acute stress responses and panic symptoms. Elevates adrenaline and cortisol. Best controlled with vitamin B1 (thiamine), which aids carbohydrate metabolism.

## Final Note

The body's stress system is meant for survival—but modern life keeps it chronically activated. Our approach targets every layer: biology, environment, nutrients, and lifestyle. When you address the full picture, stress doesn't just decrease—it stops running the show.

# The Complete Guide to Thyroid Health

Your thyroid plays a central role in your metabolism, mood, energy, and gut health — yet it's often overlooked in conventional healthcare.

## Why Thyroid Health Matters

This guide walks you through the essentials of thyroid health, symptoms of dysfunction, testing strategies, and solutions to restore optimal function.

## Common Signs of Thyroid Dysfunction

Low thyroid function (hypothyroidism) can manifest in many ways. The most frequent symptoms include:

- Constant fatigue, regardless of sleep
- Sensitivity to cold
- Low mood, depression, or anxiety

### However, hypothyroidism can contribute to a wide range of issues:

#### Gut Health
- Impaired gut health (SIBO, low motility, reduced enzyme secretion)

#### Weight Issues
- Unexplained weight gain or difficulty losing weight

#### Metabolic Health
- High cholesterol, insulin resistance and metabolic syndrome

#### Hormonal Balance
- Reduced testosterone production and sperm quality

If you have persistent health concerns without clear explanation, the thyroid is worth investigating.

## How Thyroid Hormones Are Made

Thyroid hormone production is a complex process:

1. The brain releases TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone).
2. TRH signals the pituitary gland to produce TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone).
3. TSH stimulates the thyroid gland to produce mainly T4 (inactive) and a small amount of T3 (active).
4. T4 is converted into T3 — primarily in the liver — for metabolic activity.

The problem? Many treatments only provide T4 (levothyroxine). If conversion to T3 is impaired, symptoms persist or worsen.

## Why Standard Testing Falls Short

Most doctors only test TSH. But:

- TSH can be low for reasons unrelated to thyroid hormone levels (e.g. stress).
- T4 levels may look normal even when T3 is low.
- Reverse T3 (inactive form) isn't routinely checked, yet high levels block T3 action.

### A complete thyroid assessment should include:

- TSH
- Free and total T3
- Free and total T4
- Reverse T3
- Thyroid antibodies (if autoimmunity is suspected)

## At-Home Indicators of Thyroid Health

Before modern lab testing, physicians assessed thyroid health using:

#### Morning Basal Temperature
- Consistently <97.8°F (36.6°C) suggests low thyroid.

#### Resting Pulse Rate
- Under 70 bpm may indicate hypothyroidism (if not due to fitness).

#### Achilles Reflex Test
- Slowed relaxation can point to thyroid dysfunction.

## Factors That Impair Thyroid Function

Several key factors reduce thyroid hormone activity:

#### Chronic Stress
- Increases reverse T3, impairs conversion to T3.

#### Low Carbohydrate Intake
- Reduces T3 production, increases reverse T3.

#### Calorie Restriction
- Lowers metabolic rate by decreasing T3.

#### Liver Dysfunction
- Reduces conversion of T4 to T3.

#### Inflammation
- Suppresses thyroid hormone production at multiple levels.

## Key Nutrients for Thyroid Health

#### Selenium
- Supports T4 → T3 conversion, lowers reverse T3.

#### Zinc
- Enhances conversion and reduces oxidative stress.

#### Vitamin D
- Promotes enzyme activity for T3 production.

#### Vitamin A (retinol)
- Required for thyroid hormone receptor function.

#### Iodine (from food)
- Essential for hormone synthesis, but avoid excess supplementation.

#### Leptin
- Signals energy sufficiency, promoting thyroid hormone synthesis.

## Thyroid Supplementation

Natural desiccated thyroid (NDT) — sourced from pigs or cows — contains both T4 and T3:

### Dosage Guidelines
- Typical starting dose: ½ grain (~30 mg), adjusting every 2–4 weeks.
- Goal: Morning temperature ≥97.8°F with symptom relief.

### Over-supplementation Signs
- Rapid heart rate, palpitations

## Final Thoughts

Optimizing your thyroid health requires more than just a TSH test. A holistic view — covering nutrition, stress, gut and liver health, and comprehensive lab testing — is essential for restoring metabolism, mood, and vitality.
