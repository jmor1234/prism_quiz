# Physiological Markers: What the Body Reveals

These are at-home physiological assessments Prism uses as part of its comprehensive evaluation. They provide objective data points that lab work alone often misses.

## Resting Pulse Rate

A resting pulse between 70 and 90 beats per minute indicates optimal metabolic function. A pulse consistently below this range can suggest hypothyroidism, while an elevated pulse may reflect stress hormone activity (cortisol, adrenaline). The pulse should be interpreted alongside other signs of stress, since stress hormones can artificially elevate it.

## Fingernail and Hair Quality

Dry, brittle hair and nails are often a sign of excess estrogen, which degrades connective tissue quality. These visible markers can reflect hormonal imbalances that aren't always captured by standard blood work.

## Urination Frequency

Frequent urination is often a sign of elevated "fight or flight" hormones — adrenaline, noradrenaline, and parathyroid hormone. Nighttime urination specifically can indicate disrupted anti-diuretic hormone signaling, which is influenced by stress and nutritional status.

## Foamy Urine

Fatty acids, bile salts, and certain steroid hormones can be excreted through the urine in response to stress, bacterial overgrowths, or detoxification processes, making the urine foamy. It can also result from excess protein in the urine due to kidney stress. Consistently foamy urine is a simple but telling indicator of underlying dysfunction.

## Bowel Movement Patterns

- **Infrequent bowel movements:** Driven by inadequate bowel contractions or improper stool hydration. Root causes include bacterial or fungal overgrowths, low stomach acid, nutrient deficiencies, excess serotonin, and hypothyroidism.
- **Very frequent bowel movements:** Can indicate excess bacterial growth leading to a large fecal load, or enhanced urgency driven by stress signals like adrenaline and serotonin.

## Stool Quality

- **Hard, dry stools (Bristol 1-2):** Typically associated with poor colonic hydration, which can result from nutrient deficiencies, poor motility, or inflammation from irritating foods or bacterial overgrowth.
- **Loose, watery stools (Bristol 5-7):** A key sign of inflammation or bacterial overgrowth, often driven by excess serotonin production.
- **Undigested food:** Typically indicates low stomach acid or bacterial overgrowth impairing proper digestion and assimilation.

## Morning Basal Temperature

A morning temperature consistently below 97.8°F (36.6°C) is one of the most reliable indicators of hypothyroidism — even when blood thyroid markers appear normal. This was established by Broda Barnes, MD, and remains a cornerstone of functional thyroid assessment. It reflects actual metabolic output rather than circulating hormone levels.

## Tongue Coating

A coated tongue upon waking — particularly at the back — is typically caused by fungal or bacterial overgrowth originating in the gut. It can also result from reverse peristalsis, where impaired GI motility moves microbes upward toward the mouth. This is one of the simplest visual indicators of gut dysbiosis.
