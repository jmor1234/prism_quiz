# How We Think About Evidence

The evidence hierarchy — where RCTs and meta-analyses sit on top and everything else is dismissed — was built to answer one question: _can we isolate a causal variable across a population?_ But it's often applied to answer a completely different question: _what should I do for my health?_ These are fundamentally different problems.

## Why the "Gold Standard" Has Blind Spots

**The evidence landscape is shaped by funding, not just truth.** Large long-term RCTs cost $100M+. Drug companies fund them because patents guarantee returns. No one is bankrolling a 10-year RCT on magnesium or sleep timing or a specific dietary pattern — not because these things don't work, but because no one can profit from proving they do. The absence of RCT evidence doesn't mean something is ineffective. It means no one had a financial reason to test it. The map is incomplete by design.

**RCTs measure the average, but individuals are not the average.** When an RCT reports "no significant effect," that often means some people responded strongly, others didn't, and the average washed out to nothing. This is especially true for supplements and lifestyle interventions, which tend to be targeted — they work powerfully for the people who actually need them and do little for those who don't. A targeted intervention helping 20% of people dramatically can look like noise in population data.

**Animal and cell studies are real evidence.** The core biological machinery — mitochondrial energy production, inflammatory pathways, antioxidant networks, hormone signaling — is deeply conserved across species. That's shared evolutionary architecture. These studies don't prove human outcomes, but they reveal _how the machinery works_, which is exactly what you need to reason about interventions. If something repairs mitochondrial function through a pathway that's identical in humans, that's genuine signal.

**Meta-analyses can destroy signal, not just amplify it.** If the RCTs being combined used different doses, different populations, different durations, and different outcome measures, combining them is incoherent. A meta-analysis showing "no effect" might be masking the fact that one well-designed study showed a clear effect but got diluted by three poorly designed ones. More data isn't better data when the data is measuring different things.

**Studies can only prove what they measure.** Every RCT picks specific biomarkers or outcomes to track. If an intervention improves sleep quality, reduces inflammation, and sharpens cognition — but the study only measured one blood marker that didn't move — the conclusion is "no effect." That's not a finding about reality. It's a finding about the study's measurement frame.

**"No evidence" almost never means no evidence.** When someone says "there's no evidence that X does Y," what they almost always mean is "there's no large human RCT showing this specific outcome." But there may be decades of mechanistic research, animal studies, case reports, and real-world clinical observations all pointing in the same direction. Dismissing all of that as "no evidence" is not scientific rigor — it's a narrow definition of what counts.

## The Approach That Actually Works

Use all the evidence. Mechanistic reasoning, animal data, cell studies, clinical data, population observations — each updates your probability estimate. No single type is conclusive. Together they form a usable picture. When the signal across multiple evidence types converges in the same direction, that is how rational inference under uncertainty works.

## Why Conventional Medicine Often Misses This

The evidence hierarchy isn't just a methodology — it also shapes what gets investigated, what gets funded, and what practitioners feel confident recommending. This creates real blind spots, particularly for nutritional, lifestyle, and targeted interventions that don't fit the RCT model well.

Most doctors operating within this framework are genuinely trying to help. When a doctor says "there's no evidence for that," they're usually being sincere — they're applying the standard they were trained on. But that standard was designed for pharmaceutical development, not for evaluating the full range of things that affect human health. The result is that many people with real, solvable problems get told "your labs are normal" or "there's nothing we can do" — not because their doctor doesn't care, but because the framework they're working within has structural blind spots for exactly the kind of root-cause, systems-level approach that might actually help.

This is often why people feel dismissed by conventional medicine. It's not that their doctor was wrong to follow the evidence they trust. It's that the evidence landscape itself is incomplete in ways that systematically disadvantage the interventions most likely to address root causes rather than manage symptoms.

Understanding this is important because it explains why so many people arrive at Prism having been told nothing is wrong with them — when clearly something is.
